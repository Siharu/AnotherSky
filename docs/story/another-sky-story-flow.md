# AnotherSky: 4 years or more (browser game) — Story Flow Library

A working canon map of everything built out so far. Organized top-down: universe → mechanic → story → story → open questions. Anything marked **[OPEN]** is a real gap I don't have an answer for yet — everything else reflects what's been decided in conversation.

## 1. The Universe (Transmedia Structure)

Three interconnected stories, recommended reading/playing order **Simulunas → The Beyonders → Another Sky**:

- **Simulunas** — set 2048, android survival, yellow gas / "the Great Thinning"
- **The Beyonders** — World-1, the Tartarus-2628ABH meteor, fundamental-force powers
- **Another Sky** — anchor novel/game line, World-2, Som's displacement, the Nine

Cross-world connective tissue: the yellow gas in *Simulunas* is a bleed of the same phenomenon as the **Fog of Medusa** in *Another Sky*'s world — one anomaly showing up in two otherwise-separate settings.

Existing worldbuilding constants (from before this conversation, unaffected by anything discussed today): Ghuul count is 173; the "Blank Zone" spans 2028–2031; Cygnus's signal reads *"They lied to us. Send help."*

## 2. The Core Mechanic — Save File Theory

**What it isn't:** time travel. Time travel is established as a true universal law — the one rule that cannot ever be broken, in any story, by anything.

**What it actually is:** displacement into a *parallel copy* of a world that hasn't yet caught up to "the present." Not a branch (multiverse), not divergence — **lag**. The analogy: a finished playthrough of a game doesn't erase the existence of an unplayed or half-played copy sitting on a shelf somewhere. Both exist independently; one just hasn't progressed as far.

Explicitly **not multiverse**, because multiverse implies a shared underlying "ball" — all copies coexisting simultaneously in one substrate, branching from a common point. Save File Theory has no such shared container. Copies aren't sitting next to each other in some larger space; they're at different completion-states of the same underlying process, and there's no guarantee that process is even running unless something is "playing" it.

**The "player" question:** progress in a copy isn't automatic — something has to be "playing" it forward, the way a save file only advances because someone puts hours in. This raises the unresolved question of who or what the "player" is (single entity cycling through copies? many unaware players? attention/observation itself as an abstract mechanism?). **[OPEN — deliberately left unanswered so far; worth deciding whether it should ever be answered on-page at all, or stay a mystery by design.]**

### 2.1 The Fixer (not a "chosen one")

Explicitly rejected framing: no destiny, no cosmic authorship singling anyone out.

Actual framing: a world with an unresolved situation has a "need-shaped hole." Being a **fixer** means having the skill/trait/understanding that happens to match that hole — like a key cut to a specific lock. This is **not** a mark of specialness; it's closer to a professional qualification (the AC-repair-guy analogy: the tech who shows up isn't chosen by fate, he's just the one who was qualified and available).

- Thousands of people could plausibly be shaped right to fix any given world's problem.
- Who actually gets pulled in from that qualified pool is **random draw**, not fate.
- Som was simply the **first** person ever drawn this way — "truly unlucky guy," not a destined protagonist.
- **Analogy**: Shirou Emiya's "Counter Force" pull-in from *Fate* — drawn in because a need exists and he happens to fit it, not because destiny singled him out.
- Resolution itself is *assigned*, not generic: a copy can be full of capable, willing people and still never resolve, because none of them are the specific valid key for that copy's lock. It's a permissions problem, not a difficulty problem.
- **Confirmed consistent with pre-existing novel canon**: the actual *Another Sky* lore bible (found in the `drifter-s-tale` repo, see §7) already states as a hard writing rule that "Som is not a chosen one, not a prophecy, not secretly special" — this fixer framing isn't a retcon, it's the same rule the novel was already built on.

### 2.2 Consequence of an unfinished job: permanent non-resolution

If the fixer leaves (or is removed) before finishing, the copy does **not** end, freeze, or loop. It keeps running, in real time, forever — just without ever reaching the resolution it needed. Like a movie whose protagonist never shows up to resolve the plot: the reel keeps playing, nothing after the gap is ever really "closed," and no substitute character finishing it counts, because the role was never generic to begin with.

If no other candidate is ever drawn for that copy, it just... stays broken. Indefinitely. This is explicitly **not** destruction or apocalypse — it's a colder, quieter failure: a wound that keeps almost healing and never does, for however long the copy continues to exist.

### 2.1a The No-Paradox Rule

A foundational, universe-wide rule, distinct from but related to the "no time travel" law above: **no paradox is ever permitted.**

- A living consciousness **cannot** occupy a body whose life-continuity is still unbroken/alive elsewhere — this is Som's specific case in the novel: he arrives into a body that was already alive and living in World-2, and the takeover is quiet, instant, and invisible to everyone around him (they correctly recognize "Som" because that body's life was never interrupted).
- A living consciousness **also cannot** simply arrive into a body that is dead, with no vessel to inhabit — that's the horror game protagonist's case. Since his vessel's life-continuity was broken (the body died four years ago), the world has no choice but to actively remake/physically revive the corpse first, specifically to give the arriving mind a non-paradoxical, continuous vessel to land in. This is the mechanical reason the "physical revival, not clean resurrection" detail exists (see §5.2).

### 2.3 Two Save Files, One World: Alpha and Beta

The same world-copy can itself fork into two named outcomes depending on what happens to its fixer — this is a *second, independent axis* from "how far along is this copy," layered on top of it:

- **Another Sky Alpha** — Som actually makes real progress on / finishes his part of the job. Cost: he becomes "something else" and never returns to World-1. This transformation is specifically the *price of finishing*, not a fate shared by every version of him. **Alpha is the light novel's story.**
- **Another Sky Beta** — Som finds a "crack" back to World-1 that Alpha-Som never encounters, and leaves through it before finishing. Beta is **not** a story about Som being weaker, giving up more easily, or making a worse choice — the divergence point is a single contingent thing he happens to find in one copy and not the other. Analogy explicitly given: one playthrough stumbles onto a hidden secret-boss trigger, another equally-skilled playthrough of the same game never does. No moral or effort-based weighting between the two outcomes — it's closer to a blind environmental flag than a decision.
  - **Beta is the setting of the horror game**, specifically *Another Sky: 4 years or more*.
  - Whether the "crack" has any concrete texture (a place, an object, a specific instant) or stays permanently mysterious even to Som himself is **[OPEN]**.

Both save files are understood to exist simultaneously and independently, per Save File Theory's non-multiverse logic — neither copy's inhabitants have any way of knowing the other exists, or that their world's outcome was only one of (at least) two ways it could have gone.

## 3. Story 1 — *Another Sky: Incident Zero*

- Spans 2+ years.
- Centers on **Som** — the anomaly, accidentally transferred into the world of Another Sky via a dimensional-displacement incident from his youth.
- That original incident involved **9 other people** (Som + 9 = "the Nine") who also experienced their own version of this displacement. Som can no longer remember any of them after his own transfer.
- Som arrives **before** the world's defining crisis point (the Veil of Maya, April 10th 2032) actually starts — meaning he's present for its entire unfolding, even though he never gets to finish resolving it (see §2.2/§2.3 — this is the Beta/Alpha split point).
- **[OPEN]**: how much of Incident Zero's 2+ years is "before the Veil" vs. "during" — not established yet.

## 4. The Veil of Maya

**Definition — DECIDED**: an ancient, self-protective perceptual veil humanity placed over its own eyes against monstrous/unknowable things that "puny living beings" can't handle witnessing directly. Held for thousands of years — long enough that humanity forgot it ever had the veil at all, and kept expanding/advancing (reaching the moon, etc.) underneath it without knowing. It only begins cracking after a cryptic signal is received in **2007**.

**Confirmed tie-in with the existing novel lore bible** (see §7): the bible already has a 2007 Cygnus-constellation signal detection, decoded in 2011 as *"They lied to us. Send help. Any way possible. No matter who is out there."* Your Veil of Maya framing isn't a separate mechanism competing with that signal — it's the answer to *why* the signal's arrival matters: the signal is the crack-point, the veil is the thing it cracks.

**Layering note (worth being deliberate about when actually writing scenes)**: the bible separately defines a "Blank Zone" (2028–2031, World-2) — four specific years humanity can't remember, tied to WW3's end. That's a narrower, more recent wound than the thousands-of-years-old Veil of Maya. They aren't contradictory — the Veil is the big, slow-cracking ancient cause; the Blank Zone is a sharper, localized wound inside that same unraveling — but which one explains which specific symptom on the page is worth deciding scene-by-scene rather than letting them blur together.

April 10th, 2032 (the date given early in this conversation for "when humans started seeing the unknowns" in-game) doesn't appear in the novel bible's own timeline, which instead has April 5 (Nepal outbreak begins) and April 9 (WHO declares pandemic). Worth deciding whether April 10th is the browser game's own distinct date/event, or should be reconciled with the bible's April 5/9 dates.

Two possible flavors were discussed and **left unresolved** — worth pinning down before writing the escalation arc of Map 2 / the 4-year gap:

- **[OPEN] Ontological flavor**: the unknowns were always physically present/coexisting; the Veil lifting only changes perception, not presence.
- **[OPEN] Threshold/unlock flavor**: perceiving them is what lets them start actually acting on the world — seeing and being-affected-by are the same event.
- **[OPEN]**: does the Veil's threshold stay fixed at its 2032 level, or does it keep dropping over the 4 intervening years — i.e., are things getting *worse/deeper* purely because no fixer ever closed the situation, or has it plateaued into something the world's simply learned to live inside?

Note: an earlier pass in this conversation mistakenly attributed the April 5th/Nepal-origin outbreak date to the cancelled DRIFTER project. That was wrong — the existing novel lore bible (see §7) confirms April 5/9, 2032 (Nepal outbreak / WHO pandemic declaration) as real *Another Sky* canon. This conversation's April 10th "Veil of Maya" date has not yet been reconciled with those — see §4's note on this.

## 5. Story 2 — *Another Sky: 4 years or more* (the horror game, Beta save file)

Set in **2036**, four+ years after the Veil. This is the *Another Sky Beta* save — the copy Som left unfinished.

### 5.1 The protagonist

- A new fixer-candidate, **not Som**, randomly drawn the same way Som originally was (see §2.1) — not a lesser or backup chosen one, just the next matching key pulled from the pool after the first one left.
- Arrives into a world that's had **4 years to decay** around its unresolved situation — a fundamentally harder, later, worse entry point than Som's (who arrived before anything had started).
- **Diegetic amnesia/glitchy vision**: he's an outsider, unacclimated to the Veil's effects, unlike locals who've built up 4 years of tolerance/desensitization. This is the in-fiction justification for the game's existing sanity/dread/forgetting systems running harder and faster on him than they plausibly would on a local.
- **[OPEN]**: does his glitchiness fade as the game progresses (i.e., does "improving sanity" mechanically mean "becoming acclimated like a local" — with the unsettling implication that recovery = becoming someone who's stopped seeing clearly)? Discussed as an interesting direction but never confirmed as decided canon.

### 5.2 The corpse reveal (end of Map 1)

- The **notebooks** collected throughout Map 1 (existing lore-fragment mechanic) are, in-fiction, the diary of the protagonist's **"Another Sky self"** — a person from this world who actually died four years ago.
- The protagonist's consciousness was transferred into that corpse, which had to be **physically revived** (warm blood/body) — not clean resurrection, implying possible leftover damage, reflexes, or partial something from the original body/person.
- **Reveal timing**: gated behind collecting enough notebooks — an earned, accumulation-based reveal rather than an early dump. Recontextualizes every prior NPC interaction in hindsight.
- **Reveal staging — DECIDED**: accumulation-only, no single unambiguous "gotcha" notebook. The player pieces it together themselves from stacking details across entries (dates that don't line up, handwriting that's unmistakably his, a room described as unfamiliar to him but already visited "before" he arrived) rather than being handed one final entry that spells it out outright. Deliberate choice — the story never hands the player clean answers anywhere else (the crack, the carriers' self-awareness, the world's resolution), so a spelled-out reveal here would break that pattern and underestimate the player.
- **[OPEN]**: whether the protagonist ever learns this himself (vs. it staying a reader/player-only revelation) — resolved as **yes, he learns it**, since it's explicitly the thing that ends Map 1 and moves him to Map 2. What's still open is exactly *how* the knowledge causes the transition (see 5.3).

### 5.3 Transition to Map 2 — DECIDED

After the notebook reveal, the protagonist finds an open road. His head starts hurting. He blacks out. He wakes up already in the forest, already picked up by the found-family group — no depicted cause shown for the gap in between. Deliberately left unexplained: the mystery *is* the transition. This also quietly sets the starting trust dynamic with the family — their first knowledge of him is "unconscious stranger we happened to find," not someone who walked in and introduced himself, meaning he owes them for his own intact consciousness before a word's exchanged.

### 5.4 Map 2 — The Forest

- **Not** a "silent"/empty forest — reframed as a **convergence point**: over the 4 unresolved years, all the Ghuul/husk-infected that had been spread across the wider world have migrated to and concentrated in this one forest. Very few living humans remain, encountered rarely.
- **Genre shift**: Map 2's structure moves toward *Hotel Dusk* (DS game) — dialogue/investigation-driven, character-first, contradiction-catching, rather than pure survival horror. This is a deliberate tonal pivot, not just "more forest, more monsters."
  - **[OPEN]**: whether this Hotel Dusk structure is a bounded chapter (bookended by survival-horror mechanics before/after) or persists as the dominant mode for the rest of the game.
  - **[OPEN]**: whether the game gives the player explicit tools for this (examine/press/contradiction-catching mechanics, a "does this match what they told me earlier" system) — and if so, whether those tools are reliable (clean confirmation) or deliberately unreliable (deepens unease without resolving it). Leaned toward "tools exist but don't always resolve cleanly" in discussion.
  - **[OPEN]**: whether the cast comments on the protagonist's own off-ness (glitchy, amnesiac, in a body that shouldn't be walking around) — raised as a likely and thematically appropriate beat, not confirmed as written.

### 5.5 The found-family group (~16 people)

- A survivor group who call themselves "family" — a mix of genuine blood relatives and people who joined post-collapse, roughly 16 total.
- Only a **core handful** are realistically deep-conversation cast (Hotel-Dusk-style recurring interlocutors); the rest exist as background presence.
- **Ambiguity heuristic (soft, not a hard tell)**: blood relatives who've known each other for decades are harder to fake convincingly than more recently-joined members — giving the player an intuitive but non-mechanical way to gauge risk, without a literal detector mechanic. **[OPEN]** whether the game ever gives player any harder confirmation tool beyond this soft heuristic.
- **The actual horror mechanic**: a few members are secretly, ongoingly converting the others into infected/husks over time. This is **not** a static "some are already turned" reveal — it's active and continuous, meaning the group is a worse version of itself every time the player returns to it.
- **Carrier awareness — leaned toward, not fully locked**: the carriers likely do **not** know they're doing it; the conversion may be closer to an unconscious infection-by-proximity than deliberate sabotage. This was the direction favored in discussion specifically because it removes a clean villain to blame, keeping the story's "nothing ever really resolves, no one to punish" ethos intact. **[OPEN]** as fully final canon, but currently the strongest-stated direction.

## 7. The Existing Novel Lore Bible (discovered in `drifter-s-tale` repo)

A large pre-existing canon document — `assets/Cygnus signal series Another Sky bible and others (only me and helpers should know not the readers).md` — covering the novel side of the universe (*Another Sky* + *Simulunas*) in much greater depth than this conversation had built out. Key points relevant to this doc:

- **Full Som profile**: age 28, born 2003, Bangladeshi civil engineer in Dhaka; vanishes from World-1 March 31, 2028; arrives World-2 March 26/31, 2032 (both dates appear in the bible — worth reconciling). Confirmed: "Som is not a chosen one, not a prophecy, not secretly special" is already a hard writing rule in the bible, independently consistent with this conversation's fixer framing.
- **Som's mother** becomes "Incident Zero" — the first *known* Ghuul (explicitly not the first ever), after being bitten by both a Husk and an Infected simultaneously and integrating the paradox. Kills 200+ people in a week, avoids/recognizes Som, can't fully control herself. Flagged in the bible as one of the story's emotional cores.
- **Anne** (Alessandra Nicole Almendras) existed in World-1 (Som's six-year long-distance partner) but **never existed** in World-2 — not erased, never-existed — while the *consequences* of her existence (Som meeting Alina through her) persist. The bible frames the real mystery as "how can consequences of Anne exist when Anne never existed," not "where is Anne."
- **World-2 already had its own Som** before World-1 Som's consciousness arrived — people recognize and remember him as having always lived there. The bible deliberately leaves "what happened to World-2's original Som" unresolved and instructs never to answer it inside the novel. (This conversation's No-Paradox Rule, §2.1a, explains the *mechanism* of the takeover without touching that specific unresolved mystery.)
- **Husks/Infected/Ghuuls**: Husks = total rejection of the breaking reality (9 types); Infected = partial acceptance (6 types); Ghuuls = full integration, extremely rare (~173 worldwide at the 6-month mark); Immunes = simply not-yet-exposed ordinary people (~8.28% of population at 6 months), not special, can still become any of the other three.
- **Writing-assistant rules already embedded in the bible** (apply to both novel and game work): reader only knows what Som knows; never reveal information "because it exists in the bible" or "because it sounds cool"; the outbreak/monsters/sky are all *symptoms*, not the real mystery; never invent a final answer to what humanity forgot; dreams never explain lore; every answer must create a larger question.

**Not yet reconciled between the bible and this conversation** — worth flagging rather than quietly picking one:
- Bible's outbreak timeline uses **April 5/9, 2032** (Nepal outbreak / WHO pandemic declaration); this conversation used **April 10th, 2032** for the Veil of Maya threshold. See §4.
- Bible calls the amnesia period the "Blank Zone" (2028–2031); this conversation's "Veil of Maya" is a much older, thousands-of-years standing concept. Likely two distinct, non-contradictory layers (see §4's layering note) rather than the same thing under two names, but worth being explicit about on the page.

## 8. Open Questions Worth Deciding Next

Rough priority order — the first few are the ones most likely to affect early writing/design decisions. (Map 1→2 transition and corpse-reveal staging, formerly #1-2 here, are now resolved — see §5.3 and §5.2.)

1. **Veil of Maya vs. bible timeline reconciliation** (§4, §7) — April 10th vs. April 5/9, and whether "Veil of Maya" and "Blank Zone" are meant to be the same event under two names or genuinely distinct layers.
2. **Veil escalation** (§4) — fixed threshold since 2032, or worsening — shapes how "used to it" locals actually are and what year-4 looks like compared to year-1.
3. **Carrier awareness** (§5.5) — knowing saboteurs vs. unknowing vectors; currently leaning "unknowing" but worth locking down before writing their dialogue, since it changes how they'd plausibly talk about themselves.
4. **The "crack" texture** (§2.3) — does it have any concrete form, or stay a permanent mystery even to Som?
5. **Sanity-as-acclimation** (§5.1) — whether recovering sanity mechanically doubles as "becoming like the locals" (losing clarity as a cost of stabilizing).
6. **The "player" of Save File Theory** (§2) — whether this ever gets an answer on-page at all, or is meant to stay permanently unresolved as a piece of cosmic indifference.
7. **Hotel Dusk mode's scope** (§5.4) — one chapter vs. dominant mode for the remainder of the game.
8. **World-2 original Som's fate** (§7) — the novel bible itself says never answer this on the page, so likely stays permanently open by design rather than something to "solve" here.
